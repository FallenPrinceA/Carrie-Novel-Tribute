Synopsis

Carrie is a novel written by Horror Author Stephen King. The story centers around a misunderstood 16 year old girl with a troubled path named Carrie Underwood who after having her first period starts to realize and experience psychic and telekinetic powers.

About The Characters

Carrie White

Carrie White, is an unpopular friendless and bullied high-school girl from an abusive religious household who uses her newly discovered telekinetic powers to exact revenge on those who torment her. Carrie White due to her upbringing is shown to be a bit naive and clueless regarding things even her own body. Her character turning point features a mixture of emotional highs and emotional downs. After being severly bullied, the boy of her dream asks her to prom and she is elected prom queen only to be drowned in a pool of blood before Tommy is killed by a metal bucket falling on his head. Afterwards she uses her psychic powers to enact revenge on those who torment her, which causes one of the worse natural disasters in her town's history. Carrie is the sterotypical classic anti-hero, after being bullied, tormented, and abused by her classmates in her only moment of happiness she watched her life get turned upside down in the most tragic way possible.

Margaret White

Margaret White is the mother of Carrie White, a fundamentalist christian who uses her christian views to justify torturing and abusing her daughter. Her views of religion doctrine are shown to be severly warped, such as viewing all sex including during marriage as sinful, locking Carrie in a prayer closet when she "disobeys" her, or even blaming menustration on sin.

Susan Snell

Susan is one of the few good characters in the novel. Susan after feeling guilty for what happened to Carrie in the locker room decides to pay her back by staying home on prom night to give Carrie her "special day" by having her boyfriend Tommy take Carrie to prom. Susan represents the "fairy good mother" in the story as she tries to help Carrie have her special night after all the horrible things she endured during her life. She is one of the few who survives Carrie's desturction on Prom Night however due to being crucified in public opinion, as her and Tommy are blamed for pushing Carrie to her breaking point, in the sequels it is revealed that she became a guidance counselor to atone for her sins. Carrie however at the end of the novel, believing Susan had planned this from the start digs into her mind, only to find her intentions were innocent.

Tommy Ross

Tommy is the sterotypical "Prince Charming" of the school. He is the star of the baseball team and a straight-A student. After the shower incident he agrees with his girlfriend Susan to take Carrie out to Prom, giving her the "perfect prom experience" which ends with her being elected Prom Queen. He however is killed near the end which triggers Carrie's meltdown.

Christine Hargensen

The primary antagonist of the novel and the one who torments Carrie to varying degree. She believes that Carrie is deserving of her torment and doesn't even take any responsibility for what she does, due to blaming it all on Carrie's social awkwardness and weirdness. On the night of prom she sneaks into the school during Prom night despite being banned she snuck in the night of prom and poured pig blood over Carrie and Tommy which caused the bucket to fall on his head, thus killing him and causing Carrie's rampage.

Fun Facts about Carrie By Stephen Kings

  1. Carrie is one of the most frequently banned books in United States schools, because of Carrie‚Äôs violence, cursing, underage sex and negative view of religion.
  2. Much of the book uses newspaper clippings, magazine articles, letters, and excerpts from books to tell how Carrie destroyed the fictional town of Chamberlain, Maine while exacting revenge on her sadistic classmates and her own mother Margaret.
  3. Carrie was actually King's fourth novel (the other three being Rage, The Long Walk and Blaze), but it was the first one to be published. It was written while he was living in a trailer, on a portable typewriter (on which he also wrote Misery) that belonged to his wife Tabitha. It began as a short story intended for Cavalier magazine, but King tossed the first three pages of his work in the garbage can.
